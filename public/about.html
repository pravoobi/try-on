How this works

Everything you see in the app — segmentation, pose detection, the garment warp, and (if you turn it on) depth estimation and relighting — runs as machine learning inference inside your browser tab. There is no backend for any of it: no photo or video frame is ever sent anywhere. You could disconnect from the internet after the page and its models finish loading and the try-on would keep working.

Why that's true, not just a claim

The models are downloaded once (and cached by the browser, see below), then run entirely on your device via LiteRT.js and transformers.js, both accelerated by WebGPU when your browser supports it (falling back to a Wasm/CPU path otherwise — you can toggle between the two yourself with the WebGPU / CPU switch). All of it happens in a Web Worker, off the main thread, so the page stays responsive while it infers.

This is a static site with no server-side code at all — it's hosted on GitHub Pages, which only serves files. There's nowhere for your data to go even if the app wanted to send it somewhere.

The pipeline

webcam/photo ──► [Web Worker: on-device models]
                   ├─ person segmentation ──► body mask ──────┐
                   └─ pose estimation ──► 17 keypoints ─► warp ┤
                                                               ▼
main thread ◄──────────────────── compositor (canvas 2D)
  • Segmentation finds which pixels are you, so the garment can be clipped to your silhouette and your arms/hair can occlude the fabric correctly.
  • Pose estimation finds your shoulders, waist, and hips (17 body keypoints), which is what the garment gets anchored to.
  • Thin-plate-spline warp stretches the garment's own photo onto those anchor points — this is plain geometry/math, not a model.
  • Compositor layers the warped garment over your photo/frame, masked to your silhouette.

Simple mode vs. "Enhance (3D)"

The app starts in a lightweight default mode: two small models (segmentation + pose), a few megabytes total, so it loads fast. Clicking Enhance (3D) is an explicit opt-in that downloads a larger (~30 MB) monocular depth-estimation model and unlocks:

  • Depth-tested occlusion — arms, hair, and held objects occlude the garment per-pixel, using real estimated depth instead of a simplified heuristic.
  • Relighting — the garment is shaded against a light direction estimated from your own photo, instead of keeping its original product-photo lighting baked in.
  • Orientation-aware warp (live webcam) — as you turn, the garment foreshortens, and if you've given it a back photo, turning all the way around shows the back.

Nothing in "Enhance (3D)" downloads or runs until you click the button. Turning it off returns to the simple pipeline immediately, no reload needed.

Uploading your own garment

"Upload your own garment" also runs models — background removal plus a clothes-parsing model — fully on-device, the same way. A photo of a garment on a bed or hanger gets its background stripped; a photo of someone wearing the garment additionally gets the person removed (head, arms, legs), keeping just the top or dress. The result is cropped to the garment's own silhouette, gets auto-suggested anchor points you can drag to adjust, and is saved in your browser's local storage (IndexedDB) — again, never uploaded anywhere.

Model caching

The one-time model downloads are cached by the browser (the standard Cache Storage API), keyed to this site's origin. Return later and the models load from that cache instead of downloading again — you'll notice "Enhance (3D)" reaching "ready" in a second or two on a repeat visit instead of waiting on a fresh download.

What this is

A portfolio project demonstrating on-device ML architecture in the browser: a custom Web Worker protocol, a hand-rolled thin-plate-spline solver, a canvas compositor, and an opt-in "advanced mode" layering depth estimation and relighting on top — all without a server.
